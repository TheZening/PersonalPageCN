In this post, we discussed the Larmour problem. We first introduce the orbital and spin electron magnetic moment. We first define the Bohr magneton:
\begin{equation}
  \mu_{\text{B}}=\frac{e \hbar}{2 m_{e}}
\end{equation}

>The **orbital magnetic moment** of an electron refers to the magnetic moment generated by the electron’s motion around the nucleus in an atom.

\begin{equation}
  \boldsymbol{\mu}=-g_e\frac{e}{2 m_e} \boldsymbol{L} = -g_e \mu_{\text{B}} \frac{\boldsymbol{L}}{\hbar}
\end{equation}
!!$$e=1.602 \times 10^{-19}$$ C,  a positive number!! Easy to derive:


>The **spin magnetic moment** is the magnetic moment resulting from the electron’s intrinsic angular momentum

\begin{equation}
  \boldsymbol{\mu}=-g_s \mu_{\text{B}} \frac{\boldsymbol{S}}{\hbar}
\end{equation}

We consider the spin magnetic moment only, consider a spin in an external out-of-plane magnetic field, we call $$g_s$$ as $$g$$ from now on:
\begin{equation}
  H = -\frac{1}{2} g \mu_{\text{B}} \boldsymbol{\sigma}\cdot\boldsymbol{B} = \begin{bmatrix}-g \mu_{\text{B}} B_z/2 & 0 \\\\ 0 & g \mu_{\text{B}} B_z/2
\end{bmatrix}
\end{equation}
This is a standard two-level system. The energy is lowest when the dipole moment is parallel to the field. We can compute the time evolution operator:
\begin{equation}
  U=\mqty[\exp(+i\Omega t) & 0 \\\\ 0 & \exp(-i\Omega t)] \quad \Omega=g \mu_{\text{B}} B_z/2\hbar
\end{equation}
Assuming the initial state is $$\chi(0)=(a,b)^T$$, at time $$t$$, the state is:
\begin{equation}
  \chi(t)=U\chi(0)=(a\exp(i\Omega t), b\exp(-i\Omega t))^T
\end{equation}
Now, we evaluate the expect of spin polarization vector $$\boldsymbol{S}=(S_x, S_y, S_z)$$:
\begin{equation}
   \expval{S_x}= \hbar a b \cos(2\Omega t)
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
  \expval{S_y}= -\hbar a b \sin(2\Omega t)
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
  \expval{S_z}= \frac{\hbar}{2}(a^2-b^2)
\end{equation}
For those who are more satisfied working in the langugae of a Bloch sphere, we can do the following map:
\begin{equation}
  a \to \cos\alpha/2 \quad b \to \sin\alpha/2 
\end{equation}
Then:

$$
\begin{align}
  \expval{S_x}=& \frac{\hbar}{2} \cos\alpha \cos(2\Omega t) \\
  \expval{S_y}=& -\frac{\hbar}{2} \sin\alpha \cos(2\Omega t) \\
  \expval{S_z}=& \frac{\hbar}{2}\cos\alpha
\end{align}
$$

This is suggesting that the expectation of $$S_x, S_y$$ will rotate around the z-axis with frequency $$2\Omega$$! That is the reason why we call it Larmour precession.
